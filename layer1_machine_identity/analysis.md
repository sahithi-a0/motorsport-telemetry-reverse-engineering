Plot 1 — Speed Over Time
The speed trace across the session runs for 13 minutes and 52 seconds, and the pattern is immediately readable. Every peak is a straight, every trough is a corner — and as the laps progress, those peaks climb slightly as the driver builds confidence and starts to trust the car more.
Top speed recorded is 193 km/h. That's the absolute ceiling this track's longest straight allows. On the other end, minimum corner speed drops to 5.1 km/h — that's essentially a standing start out of a hairpin. Tells you straight away this track has at least one very unforgiving slow corner where braking precision is everything.
One thing worth flagging is the GPS speed versus vehicle speed delta. The maximum gap between the two reaches 7.27 km/h at points, but the session average is only 1.534 km/h. That small average difference is actually reassuring — it means there's no persistent wheel slip. The occasional spikes are brief corner-exit wheelspin under hard acceleration, which is entirely expected behaviour.
Lap 9 stands out clearly as an anomaly. The speed drops and stays suppressed — likely a yellow flag or a deliberate cool-down lap. It's a clean break in an otherwise consistent wave pattern.

Plot 2 — RPM Trace (Best Lap)
The RPM trace through the best lap tells you how hard the engine is being worked, and the short answer is — hard, almost constantly. The rev limiter sits at 6,548 rpm, and the trace flatlines there on every straight without exception. The driver is extracting the full rpm ceiling before each upshift, every single time.
Average RPM across the 71.2-second best lap comes out at 4,993 rpm. That's a high average — there's almost no idle time, no coasting. The engine is in its working range for the overwhelming majority of the lap.
The lowest RPM recorded in the best lap is 3,018 rpm. That's the tightest corner on the track — the driver has braked as hard as the car allows and scrubbed the speed down to almost nothing. Even there, the driver stays in second gear and doesn't drop to first, which is an important detail.
Counting the RPM dips below 4,500 rpm, there are 6 clear braking events in the best lap. Six corners where the engine load drops meaningfully. The rest of the lap — roughly 81% of it — the engine is spinning hard and the driver is fully committed.

Plot 3 — RPM vs Speed (Gearbox Map)
Opening this plot, five diagonal stripes appear naturally from the data without any labelling. Each stripe is a gear revealing its own mechanical fingerprint. The steeper the stripe, the shorter the gear ratio — accelerates quickly but runs out of speed fast. A flatter stripe means a longer gear — slower to rev up but carries the car to higher speeds.
Gear 1 has a slope of 151 rpm per km/h — extremely steep. It only covers a 10 km/h window between 66 and 76 km/h, which makes it almost irrelevant on this track. The driver barely visits it, and in the best lap, doesn't use it at all.
Gear 2 is the workhorse. Slope of 53.6 rpm per km/h, covering 56 to 117 km/h — the widest speed range of any gear at 61 km/h wide. That's the gear this track is built around, and the data proves it.
Moving up, Gear 3 covers 81 to 147 km/h with a slight overlap into Gear 2's range — that's the shift point zone. Gears 4 and 5 are closely spaced at the top, with slopes of 34.8 and 32.4 respectively. The upper gears are almost identical in ratio, which is typical — the gearbox is optimised for low-speed acceleration out of corners with closely packed top gears for straight-line maintenance. Gear 5 tops out at 193 km/h at 6,142 rpm, which is just below the rev limiter. The driver runs out of straight before the engine reaches its ceiling — 5th is slightly too long for this track's straight length.

Plot 4 — Gear Staircase (Best Lap)
The staircase plot confirms everything the RPM trace suggested, and puts a time stamp on each gear. The car spends 37.1 seconds — 52% of the best lap — in second gear. More than half the lap in one gear. That single number tells you everything about this circuit's character. It's a slow, technical track where lap time is found in the braking zones and corner exits, not on the straights.
Third gear accounts for 18.4 seconds, about 26% of the lap. Fourth and fifth each take roughly 8 seconds, covering the main straight and the brief high-speed sections that follow. Gear 1 doesn't appear in the best lap at all.
The total gear change count across the 71.2-second lap is 16. That's one gear change every 4.5 seconds on average. For context, a high-speed power circuit might see 6 or 7 gear changes per lap. 16 is busy — the driver's inputs are constant, there's barely a moment of passive cruising anywhere.
The staircase shape itself is clean. The sequence climbs 2→3→4→5 on the main straight, then cliff-drops back to 2 in a single braking zone. That cliff is the hardest braking point on the track — the driver is shedding three gears almost simultaneously. And critically, the speed trace overlaid in blue mirrors the gear steps exactly. No hesitation, no missed shifts — the technique is tidy.

Conclusion
Pulling all four plots together, the picture is clear. This is a low-speed, high-technical circuit with a short main straight, at least six meaningful braking corners, and a car that lives in second gear for over half its best lap. The engine is being worked close to its rev ceiling consistently, and the driver's technique — particularly the shift discipline and gear change timing — is clean throughout.
From the data alone, without any external car knowledge, what emerges is a small, lightweight, naturally aspirated machine. The 193 km/h top speed is modest by circuit racing standards. The 5-speed gearbox with closely spaced top ratios, combined with an average RPM of nearly 5,000 across the best lap, points toward a free-revving engine that stays responsive at all speeds. The average GPS-to-vehicle speed delta of 1.5 km/h suggests the car has decent mechanical grip for its power output — no persistent traction problem.
If I had to put a category on it from the numbers alone — a Formula 4 or similar entry-level open-wheel single seater. Light, technical, built entirely around corner performance. A car where the driver makes the difference, not the horsepower.
