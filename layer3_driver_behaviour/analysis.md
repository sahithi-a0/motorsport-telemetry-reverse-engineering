## ANALYSIS
## LAYER-3- Each Driver’s Driving Style

# Driver-3
# Plot 1 — Driver Inputs Trace (Speed, Throttle, Brake, Steering)
The inputs trace is where the lap stops being a number and starts being a story. Four channels — speed, throttle, brake, steering — plotted against lap distance. Everything the driver does physically shows up here. Reading it well means understanding what each channel is actually measuring versus what it's responding to. Throttle position is something the driver controls directly. Speed is what happens as a result.
Looking at the speed channel first. Driver 5 leads on every metric — top speed of 198 km/h, minimum corner speed of 61 km/h, and an average lap speed of 114 km/h. Driver 4 runs 194 km/h top speed with a 54 km/h minimum, averaging 112 km/h. Driver 3 tops out at 193 km/h with a 59 km/h minimum and 110 km/h average. That 4 km/h difference in average speed between Driver 5 and Driver 3, sustained over 2,154 metres, directly accounts for the 2.7 second gap between their best qualifying times of 1:08.5 and 1:11.2. The average speed number is the clearest single indicator of overall pace.
The throttle channel throws up something that looks counter-intuitive at first. Driver 4 is on full throttle for 28.8 seconds — 41% of the lap. Driver 3 for 27.1 seconds — 38%. Driver 5, the fastest of the three, is at fully saturated throttle for only 2.1 seconds. That's 3% of the lap. The instinct is to assume the fastest driver should be on the power the most, but that's not what's happening here. Driver 5 is modulating continuously — partial throttle blended with lateral load through the corners, managing combined slip rather than waiting for a clean straight to apply full power. It's a more demanding technique to execute but a more efficient one in terms of lap time.
The brake channel needs a flag before any conclusions are drawn. Driver 3 shows brake position above 5% for 67.9 seconds out of a 71.2 second lap — that's 95% of the lap with the brake pedal partially depressed. Physically, that doesn't hold up for this circuit. What it almost certainly indicates is a sensor zero offset — a small mechanical preload sitting in the pedal position sensor that keeps the reading artificially elevated at rest. This is a data quality issue and it needs to be noted. It doesn't invalidate the brake pressure analysis in Plot 6, which reads hydraulic pressure at the caliper independently, but it does mean Driver 3's brake position numbers cannot be taken at face value. Driver 4's 26.0 seconds and Driver 5's 23.4 seconds on brake are both physically plausible for this layout.
On steering, Driver 3 reaches 90 degrees right and 80 degrees left. Driver 5 shows 103 degrees right and 97 degrees left — the most symmetric of the three, which suggests the most balanced line through left and right hand corners. Driver 4 records an anomalous 176 degree value on the right. That's physically impossible for a conventional rack-and-pinion system. It confirms a sensor scaling or calibration issue on Driver 4's steering channel, which needs to be resolved before that data can be used for technique analysis.

# Plot 2 — Trail Braking Gate
Trail braking is the technique of holding brake pressure into corner entry while simultaneously beginning to steer. Done correctly, it progressively loads the front axle, increases front grip, and allows a later apex. It's a transient technique — it operates in the transition phase between braking and cornering — not something that should be active for large portions of a lap.
In qualifying, Driver 3 registers trail braking for 45.4 seconds across 33 zones — 64% of the lap. That number is again a direct consequence of the brake sensor offset identified in Plot 1. When the pedal position sensor reads above 5% at rest and the throttle is also above 5%, the logical gate that detects trail braking activates continuously. The genuine trail braking events for Driver 3 are the longer, sustained zones visible on the distance trace — not the short scattered flicker events.
Driver 4 trail brakes for 5.1 seconds — 7% of the lap — despite having the most braking zones detected across the session. Driver 5 registers 3.5 seconds — 5%, across 16 zones. Both of those numbers point toward a point-and-squirt technique: brake hard and straight, get the rotation done, then commit to the throttle. It prioritises traction at corner exit over minimum speed at the apex. On a circuit with this many tight hairpins, that's a defensible approach.
In the race, Driver 4's trail braking count jumps sharply — from 5.1 seconds across 37 zones in qualifying to 34.1 seconds across 69 zones. That near-doubling of zones detected tells you the braking inputs became significantly more varied under race conditions, likely from traffic management or tyre degradation changing what corner entry required. Driver 5 stays at 2.5 seconds — 3% — consistent and disciplined across both sessions.

# Plot 3 — Throttle Derivative
The throttle derivative isn't telling you what the throttle is doing — it's telling you how fast it's changing. That distinction matters. A high positive rate is an aggressive snap open. A high negative rate is a sudden lift. Together they give you the aggression signature of each driver's throttle technique.
In qualifying, Driver 5 records the highest closing rate at -1,060 %/s — the most decisive lift of the three. When Driver 5 comes off the throttle, it happens almost instantaneously. Full commitment to the braking event, no hesitation. Their maximum opening rate is 630 %/s. Driver 3's opening rate is the slowest at 394 %/s, with a closing rate of -928 %/s. Driver 4 sits in between at 505 %/s opening and -990 %/s closing.
The mean opening rate tells you more about lap time than the peak values. Driver 5's mean of 47 %/s is the lowest of the three — lower than Driver 3's 71 %/s and Driver 4's 54 %/s. A lower mean opening rate means Driver 5 builds throttle more gradually on average. That's entirely consistent with the partial throttle technique seen in Plot 1 — managing combined slip at corner exit rather than snapping to full power and risking wheelspin. The faster application rates from Driver 3 and Driver 4 suggest a more binary on-off throttle style.
In the race, Driver 3's maximum opening rate jumps to 733 %/s — significantly more aggressive than their qualifying technique. The mean drops to 54 %/s though, which means those aggressive spikes are concentrated at specific corners rather than a change in general style. It could be tyre degradation forcing more binary inputs, or overtaking attempts demanding faster corner exit acceleration. Driver 4 and Driver 5 both remain broadly similar between qualifying and race — more repeatable technique, less reactive to conditions.

# Plot 4 — GG Diagram
The GG diagram is the performance envelope. Lateral acceleration plotted against longitudinal acceleration for every data point in the lap. A driver fully exploiting the tyre's combined grip limit produces a circular or diamond-shaped cloud that fills the theoretical boundary. A driver leaving grip unused shows a cloud that doesn't reach it.
Driver 3 in qualifying shows a lateral peak of 5.890g and maximum braking deceleration of 5.410g. The 98th percentile grip envelope sits at 5.314g — high, consistent with a formula-style car generating meaningful aerodynamic downforce at speed. Mean combined G across the lap is 2.277g, meaning Driver 3 uses an average of 43% of peak grip capacity throughout the lap.
Driver 4's grip envelope in qualifying is 2.562g — significantly lower than Driver 3's 5.314g, with both drivers in the same car. That is the most concerning data point in the entire Layer 3 analysis. Either the accelerometer channels on Driver 4's car carry a scaling error — plausible given the brake and steering sensor issues already identified — or Driver 4 is genuinely not loading the car through the corners. A mean combined G of 1.124g against Driver 3's 2.277g is too large a gap to be attributed to driving style alone. The sensor calibration needs to be checked before any conclusions are drawn from Driver 4's GG data.
Driver 5 sits at 2.809g envelope and 1.206g mean — similar in magnitude to Driver 4, which raises the same calibration question. But Driver 5 is the fastest driver in the session. If those G values are genuine, then Driver 5 is generating the fastest lap time with the least peak grip loading — which is physically possible through very smooth corner transitions and minimal combined slip events. It would actually reinforce everything the throttle and slip ratio data already suggests about their technique.
In the race, all three drivers show reduced envelopes against qualifying. Expected — tyre degradation, fuel load changes, the tyres simply not operating at the same peak level. Driver 3 drops from 5.314g to 5.185g. Driver 4 and Driver 5 both hold near their qualifying values. The consistency of those low G readings across both sessions for Driver 4 and Driver 5 makes the sensor calibration question harder to dismiss.

# Plot 5 — Steering Derivative
The steering derivative shows whether corner entry is smooth or aggressive. A smooth driver produces a sustained, moderate turn-in rate with gradual unwinding. An aggressive driver shows sharp spikes at turn-in and fast unwinding at the apex — what engineers call a low steering smoothness index.
Driver 3 in qualifying shows a maximum steering rate of 631 deg/s to the right and -1,200 deg/s to the left. The left side rate is nearly double the right. That level of asymmetry is significant — Driver 3 is substantially more aggressive turning into left-hand corners than right-hand corners. On a circuit where the primary braking zones feed into left-hand hairpins, there may be a deliberate rotation strategy behind it, but it also raises the possibility of a tendency toward transient oversteer on left-hand entry. Something worth watching in the video if it's available.
Driver 4 shows 592 deg/s right and -562 deg/s left — the most symmetric of the three. Balanced, neutral inputs into both directions. Driver 5 reads 649 deg/s right and -532 deg/s left, with a mean absolute rate of 52 deg/s. The time above 200 deg/s — the aggressive input threshold — is broadly similar across all three drivers at 3.2 to 4.2 seconds per lap. So the number of aggressive steering events is comparable. What differs is how extreme those events are when they happen.
In the race, Driver 3's steering asymmetry reverses completely. Maximum rate to the right jumps to 1,310 deg/s while the left drops to -643 deg/s — a full inversion of the qualifying pattern. Race traffic could explain it, different corner priorities under defensive driving. Another possibility is tyre degradation affecting one side of the car more than the other, changing the balance and requiring more aggressive input on the side that was previously smooth to achieve the same rotation.

# Plot 6 — Brake Pressure Delta
The brake pressure channel is the most reliable braking metric in this dataset. It measures actual hydraulic force at the caliper — independent of the pedal position sensor issues that affected Plots 1 and 2. These numbers can be trusted.
Driver 5 produces the highest peak brake pressure at 39.70 bar. That's harder than Driver 3 at 36.09 bar and Driver 4 at 34.91 bar. Consistent with Driver 5 braking later and arriving at corner entry with more speed. Their mean active brake pressure of 12.44 bar is also the highest — not just a harder peak, but sustained threshold braking held through the full deceleration phase rather than a quick spike and release.
Driver 3's mean active pressure tells a different story at 4.30 bar — significantly lower than Driver 5's 12.44 bar despite a similar peak. That pattern describes a driver who hits the brakes hard at the initial application and then quickly releases pressure, rather than holding threshold through the middle of the braking zone. The car decelerates efficiently at the start of braking and then less so. It's a common technique issue — the instinct to release the pedal once the initial load is in, rather than trusting the threshold and holding it.
Driver 4 shows 10 braking zones detected in qualifying against Driver 5's 8. More events in the same lap length means Driver 4 is splitting what the other drivers treat as a single braking event into multiple applications — building, partially releasing, reapplying. It's a legitimate technique for managing front-end balance on corner entry, but it's less efficient than clean threshold braking held through the zone.
In the race, Driver 4's peak rises to 36.45 bar and the number of braking zones increases to 13 — consistent with the increased trail braking activity in Plot 2, more complex application patterns under race conditions. Driver 5's peak drops considerably to 26.61 bar from their 39.70 bar qualifying peak. Whether that's tyre management, a deliberate strategy to reduce brake stress over the full race distance, or a braking point change that allows a lower peak while maintaining corner entry speed — that would need the stint data to confirm.

# Plot 7 — Wheel Slip Ratio
The slip ratio is a derived channel — vehicle speed minus GPS speed, divided by GPS speed, expressed as a percentage. Positive values are wheelspin, driven wheels rotating faster than actual ground velocity. Negative values are lockup, wheels rotating slower than the car is travelling during braking.
In qualifying, Driver 4 produces the highest peak wheelspin at 10.17% but the shortest duration above the 3% threshold at 11.2 seconds. Driver 3 peaks lower at 9.28% but sustains it the longest — 14.8 seconds above threshold. Driver 5 is the most efficient across both metrics: lowest peak at 8.32% and 12.0 seconds above threshold.
What makes Driver 5's numbers meaningful isn't just the lower peak — it's the combination of lower peak, shorter duration, and faster recovery. Sustained wheelspin above 3% is energy going into tyre heat and wear rather than forward velocity. Driver 3's 14.8 seconds of significant wheelspin in a 71.2 second lap means roughly 21% of the lap the driven wheels aren't operating at peak traction efficiency.
On the lockup side, all three drivers show brief spikes in the heaviest braking zones — normal behaviour. None are carrying sustained lockup events. Driver 5 peaks at -5.04% for 1.0 second, Driver 3 at -4.76% for 0.7 seconds, Driver 4 at -4.66% for 1.2 seconds. Short, sharp, and recovered quickly.
The location of Driver 3's peak wheelspin is the detail that stands out. That 9.28% peak occurs at a throttle position of only 26% — mid-corner, not on the straight under full acceleration. The car is breaking traction under partial throttle while still carrying lateral load. That's a combined slip condition, and it's a specific problem with a specific set of solutions — either a differential calibration change to reduce locking under partial throttle, or a throttle map adjustment that reduces torque delivery during the mid-corner phase. It's not something the driver can fully manage on their own without a mechanical change underneath them.

The tyre is predominantly loaded in the cornering direction — not in braking, not in straight-line acceleration. Both of those peak lower than the lateral G. The car is always most stressed through corners, never under braking or straight acceleration.
The densest part of the cloud sits in the upper right quadrant — accelerating while turning right, which means exiting left-hand hairpins onto the straights. Orange and yellow points in the 120 to 160 km/h range. These are corner exits where Driver 5 is accelerating and still turning simultaneously. That's the combined slip technique showing up as physical tyre loading data — time spent in a quadrant that a pure point-and-squirt approach barely touches.
Mean combined G of 1.09g is almost identical to Driver 4's 1.06g. Same average tyre loading across the lap. What's different is the direction. Driver 5's average shifts toward lateral. Driver 4's is more even. Same workload on the tyre, different type of work.

# Plot 5 — Steering Derivative
The raw trace in the upper panel is the most visually immediate story of any single plot across all three drivers. Look at the first 400 metres — the opening corner sequence. The steering trace shows a series of undulations: right, slightly left, right, left, right. The wheel never fully returns to zero between them. Driver 5 is treating the opening section as one flowing connected sequence rather than a series of separate turns. The car is always being guided through something, never just pointed straight between events.
From 500 to 800 metres the steering holds a sustained right-hand angle — a long gentle arc sitting at 20 to 30 degrees through what the Layer 2 analysis confirmed as a medium-radius sweeper. No sharp turn-in, no aggressive unwinding. Just a smooth consistent hold.
The 800 to 1,000 metre section is the most active — multiple direction changes through the chicane with the blue left-fill reaching its deepest point around 900 metres. That's the tightest left-hand corner on this section of the circuit and it gets the most aggressive input on the lap. Expected and appropriate.
From 1,000 to 1,600 metres the steering settles into a sustained left — blue fill at moderate angles held consistently through the long left-hand sweeper that follows the chicane.
Maximum right rate 449 deg/s, maximum left -532 deg/s. Left is 18% higher — a mild asymmetry that traces entirely back to that one chicane entry at 900 metres. One aggressive input at the geometrically tightest corner is the right approach, not a weakness. Mean absolute rate of 42 deg/s is the lowest of the three. Aggressive input time above 200 deg/s is 2.6 seconds — similar to Driver 4's 2.3 and significantly less than Driver 3's 4.25 seconds.
One thing in Driver 5's panel that neither other driver shows — between corners, on the straights, the derivative returns cleanly to near zero with almost nothing happening. Driver 5 isn't moving the wheel on the straights. Not micro-correcting, not making small adjustments. The steering just sits still. That tells you the car is well balanced and the driver trusts it completely on the straight.

# Plot 6 — Brake Pressure Delta
Five braking zones. That's the number to start with. Driver 4 had ten zones on the same circuit in the same car. Driver 5 does the same lap in five braking events. That single comparison tells you more about the difference in technique than almost anything else in this dataset.
Two dominant events dominate the raw pressure trace. First at roughly 150 to 250 metres reaching 39.70 bar, second at around 650 to 800 metres reaching 37.10 bar. Both are the highest pressure events in the entire qualifying dataset across all three drivers. And both are notably wide — the pressure sustains near the peak for longer than any equivalent event in Driver 4's trace. Clean mountain shape — quick build, extended flat near the top, progressive release. Textbook threshold braking.
Third and fourth zones at 908 metres (21.5 bar) and 519 metres (19.9 bar) are moderate chicane applications. The fifth at 1,537 metres barely registers at 12.9 bar — a light touch. Five zones, each a single committed event with no reapplication.
Look at the bar chart and it's immediately clean. Five well-separated bars across the lap. No clustering, no secondary events sitting next to primary ones, no evidence of the press-release-press-again approach that gave Driver 4 ten zones. Just five clear decisions.
39.70 bar at 193 metres is the hardest single braking event in the entire qualifying dataset. Driver 5 arrives at that corner faster than anyone and brakes harder than anyone. That combination is only possible with a later braking point. Arriving at higher speed requires either braking later, braking harder, or both. Driver 5 does both.
Mean active pressure of 9.31 bar sits slightly below Driver 4's 9.90 bar. The lighter chicane applications pull the mean down. If you looked at only the two primary zones the mean would be considerably higher.

# Plot 7 — Wheel Slip Ratio
Vehicle speed and GPS speed run almost on top of each other through most of the lap. Maximum vehicle speed 190 km/h, maximum GPS speed 189 km/h — a 1 km/h gap at peak. Maximum difference across the full lap is 5.9 km/h — the smallest of any driver. The two lines are visually almost identical through the straights and clean corners.
The gaps between them appear at two places — around 200 to 350 metres and around 1,700 to 1,900 metres. Both corner exits following the two primary braking events. Those are the only visible gaps. Driver 5's traction loss is concentrated at two specific predictable positions, not scattered around the lap.
Maximum wheelspin of 8.32% — the lowest peak of the three. But wheelspin time above 3% is 14.0 seconds, which is actually higher than Driver 4's 11.7 seconds. That needs explaining.
The character of the wheelspin is completely different. Driver 4 shows sharp isolated spikes where the tyre suddenly loses traction. Driver 5 shows broad sustained mounds — a controlled low-level slip held through the entire corner exit ramp. Instead of running at zero slip through the corner then spiking when full throttle goes on, Driver 5 holds a steady 2 to 3% slip throughout the throttle build phase. The tyre is always operating near the traction limit, never violently over it. That's exactly how the combined slip technique is supposed to look in the data. The 14 seconds is higher because more of the lap is spent deliberately near the traction limit — not because Driver 5 is losing control of traction more often.
Maximum lockup of -3.77% for just 0.2 seconds — the shortest lockup duration of any driver. The blue lockup signal is barely visible on this plot. One thin near-zero baseline from the GPS geometry artefact, one brief real excursion. Driver 5 almost never locks a wheel.
Mean absolute slip of 1.72% is actually the highest of the three drivers. In isolation that seems wrong for the fastest driver. In context it makes complete sense. The higher mean comes from spending more of the lap operating deliberately near the traction limit rather than cycling between far below it and far above it. More average slip means more time near the limit. That's the right place to be.

# Conclusion
### Driver 3 — Committed but Disconnected

If you were standing at the side of the track watching Driver 3 go past, you'd think they were the fastest. The steering is always moving, the throttle snaps hard in both directions, the brakes go on with real aggression. It looks like a driver who is absolutely wringing the car's neck. And that's exactly what makes the data so confusing when you first look at it.

Because none of that activity is translating the way it should.

Take the steering. More movement than Driver 5, yet the mean absolute angle ends up at 30.0 degrees — almost identical to Driver 4. The reason is that a lot of those steering inputs aren't planned turn-ins, they're corrections after something didn't go the way it was supposed to. Same story with the braking — 36.09 bar peak, which is hard, genuinely hard — but the mean active pressure drops to just 4.30 bar because the brake goes on and immediately starts coming back off. The deceleration zone isn't being used. The hard initial hit is doing the work and then the pedal is already backing off before the car has finished slowing down.

And the wheelspin. 14.8 seconds above the 3% threshold, more than anyone, and slower corner exits than Driver 5. More wheelspin, less speed. That combination tells you everything.

What's actually happening is that Driver 3 goes into each phase of the corner with real intensity — and then doesn't hold it. The commitment is there at the beginning. It just doesn't last long enough to extract anything from it. The brake hits hard and releases. The throttle goes on and gets broken up by wheelspin. The steering attacks the left-hand entries at -1200 deg/s and then the car rotates more than expected and suddenly there are corrections to make. The same pattern, over and over.

That left-right asymmetry in the steering is worth dwelling on. -1200 deg/s to the left, 631 deg/s to the right. Driver 3 is attacking left-hand entries nearly twice as aggressively as right-hand ones. On a circuit where the big braking zones all feed left-hand hairpins, that probably feels like the right thing to do from inside the car. In the data it looks like a driver who keeps arriving at the most important corners slightly too hot and then having to deal with the consequences.

The wheelspin location is probably the single most important detail in this whole dataset when it comes to understanding Driver 3. It peaks at 26% throttle, mid-corner, while the car is still loaded laterally. This isn't wheelspin going down a straight. This is wheelspin happening in the middle of a corner while the tyre is already working hard to keep the car turning. Driver 3 is asking the tyre to hold cornering grip and build traction at the same time, under partial throttle, in the exact phase of the corner where the tyre is least able to do both. The result is heat. More heat than anyone. Oil temperatures up 30.4°C across the race — the highest temperature rise per unit of performance in the dataset. The car is working incredibly hard and going slower than it should.

The honest summary is that this is a driving style that was probably built on a different type of circuit. Long straights, separate corners, enough room between each event to reset and set up for the next one. At Kari, the next corner is within 200 metres of every single exit. There's no room to reset. The technique never gets to operate the way it was designed to, and every sensor in the car is showing you that friction at the same time.

### Driver 4 — Precise but Sequential

Driver 4 is incredibly tidy. That's the first thing you notice. The lap is broken into clean phases — brake, turn, accelerate — and each one is executed with real care before moving on to the next. Within each of those phases, the technique is genuinely impressive. The issue is what happens in the tiny gaps between them.

Look at the throttle trace and it's almost perfectly binary. Flat out for 26.3 seconds, completely off for 23.3 seconds, with barely anything in between — only 30% of the lap at any partial position. The openings are fast at 505 %/s and the closings are decisive at -1040 %/s. No messing around. The braking data — the position channel is corrupted but the pressure data is fine — shows a driver who holds threshold pressure through each zone, averaging 9.90 bar mean active. That's the best threshold technique in this dataset. Clean, sustained, proper.

The steering is balanced almost perfectly. 592 deg/s right, 562 deg/s left. Barely 1.04:1. Mean absolute rate of 45 deg/s. Fewest aggressive inputs of the three at just 2.3 seconds above 200 deg/s. Driver 4 makes hardly any corrections because the initial inputs are accurate enough that corrections aren't needed. The car does what it's told and goes where it's pointed. That's a real skill and it shows up clearly in the data.

But here's the problem with the sequential approach on this particular circuit. Every time Driver 4 finishes one phase and moves to the next, there's a brief moment where nothing is really happening — the car isn't braking, isn't turning, isn't accelerating. It's transitioning. On a track with long straights that moment costs nothing because there's space to absorb it. At Kari, that moment is time that isn't being used, lap after lap after lap.

The wheelspin at corner exit tells you exactly what happens when the throttle finally opens. 12.07% maximum, highest in qualifying, because the throttle has been held at zero all the way through the loaded phase and when it eventually opens the tyre just isn't ready. It's still dealing with lateral load and suddenly it's also being asked to accelerate. That snap — clean and decisive in isolation — is actually the cost of not managing the transition gradually.

The ten braking zones versus Driver 5's five is the same story from the braking side. Driver 4 has to brake, partially release, and reapply through the chicane sequences rather than carrying momentum through them. Each of those release-reapply cycles disturbs the car, costs a little time, and adds up. Ten zones where five were enough is where most of that 1.3 second gap actually lives.

The GG diagram is round and well distributed. A genuinely balanced driver who uses grip in all directions consistently. This isn't an inconsistency problem. Driver 4 is extremely consistent. The problem is that even perfect sequential execution leaves gaps that a driver managing everything simultaneously doesn't have to deal with.

### Driver 5 — Continuous and Connected

Before you've looked at anything other than the speed trace, something is already different. The speed valleys — the slowest point at each corner — are shallower than the other two. Not slightly shallower. Noticeably, consistently shallower at every corner on the circuit. And then you look at the throttle trace and it's sitting below both of the others for almost the entire lap, and suddenly the two things make sense together.

0.7 seconds of full throttle. 1% of the lap. And the fastest driver by 1.3 seconds.

That number takes some time to sit with. The immediate instinct is that it can't be right, or that full throttle must not be what matters, or that there's something wrong with the data. None of those are true. What's actually happening is that full throttle was never really the point. Getting corner exit speed is the point. Full throttle is only relevant in the window between the corner fully releasing and the next braking zone. Driver 5 has been accelerating through partial throttle for several hundred metres before that window even opens. By the time flat out would be possible, the straight is basically over. There's nowhere left to use it.

What you see in the throttle trace at each exit is a gradual ramp climbing from zero up toward 80 or 90 percent. That ramp is Driver 5 reading how much lateral load is still on the tyre and opening the throttle at exactly the rate the tyre can absorb without losing traction. Still turning — ramp stays gentle. Car starts to straighten — ramp steepens. It's a continuous adjustment, every exit, every corner, every lap. Nothing is fixed. Nothing is pre-planned in the way Driver 4's binary on-off approach is pre-planned.

The numbers behind it are the maximum opening rate of 630 %/s — highest of the three — combined with a mean opening rate of 42 %/s — lowest of the three. High peak, low average. That's the shape of a driver who is mostly being smooth and measured and then occasionally very sharp and decisive at the exact right moment. Not slow everywhere, not aggressive everywhere. Slow where it protects the tyre and sharp where it gains time. And the lift at the end — -1060 %/s closing rate, also the highest — is instant and total. No gradual backing off when the braking point arrives. The commitment in both directions is absolute.

The braking: five zones, 39.70 bar peak, hardest single braking event across all three drivers in the whole qualifying dataset. Driver 5 arrives faster at every corner than either of the others, brakes harder, does it in one clean committed event. The later braking point was earned by a better exit from the corner before. Which was earned by a better exit from the corner before that. It compounds. Every corner makes the next corner slightly better and the time advantage builds with it.

The GG diagram shows the whole thing as a shape. The cloud pushes further into the combined acceleration-and-cornering zone than either of the others. More time on power while still turning. That's the zone that a point-and-squirt technique barely visits — you have to be doing both things at once to spend time there — and Driver 5 lives in it more than anyone else in this dataset.

The steering is the quietest. 109 degrees maximum right, 97 left. Mean absolute angle of 25.1 degrees, lowest of the three. Higher corner speeds with less steering movement. That's not being cautious — that's a better line. Turning in earlier, holding a wider arc, arriving at the apex with less lock required because less rotation was needed to get there. On the straights there's almost nothing happening in the steering at all. No micro-corrections, no fidgeting. The car is completely trusted on the straight because what happened in the corner before the straight was done properly.

### The One-Sentence Portrait of Each Driver

**Driver 3** is working harder than anyone in the car and getting the least back for it — high intensity, hard inputs, more heat and wheelspin than either of the others, and lap times that don't reflect any of that effort.

**Driver 4** is technically excellent within each individual phase of the lap but loses time in the transitions between them — a clean, disciplined approach that would work well on a lot of circuits but is slightly mismatched to one where corners arrive this quickly.

**Driver 5** has no phases — the throttle, brakes and steering are all active at the same time through every corner, the tyre is managed as one shared resource rather than separate jobs done one at a time, and the lap time is just what happens when you never lose the speed that the other two spend the whole straight trying to get back.
