# Plot 5 — Throttle and Brake Overlay
The throttle overlay is where the most counter-intuitive finding in this entire qualifying dataset becomes visible. Driver 5's throttle trace sits lower on the chart than either of the other two for almost the entire lap. Driver 3 and Driver 4 both show sustained periods at full throttle. Driver 5 barely touches the ceiling.
Full throttle time — Driver 4 at 28.8 seconds, Driver 3 at 27.1 seconds, Driver 5 at 2.1 seconds. The fastest driver uses the least full throttle. Every time I show that number to someone who hasn't seen the data, they assume it's a mistake.
Look at what's happening in the corner zones and it makes sense. Driver 3 and Driver 4 both show a binary pattern — throttle drops near zero in the braking zone, stays low through the apex, then snaps up on exit. Driver 5's trace never fully drops to zero and never fully saturates to 100% through the same zones. It sits in the 30 to 70% range through corners, continuously adjusting. Driver 5 is managing the tyre load in real time rather than waiting for a clean state before applying power.
The brake overlay adds the other side of the picture. The key observation isn't the total brake time — it's when each driver starts the throttle again after braking. Driver 5 consistently begins throttle re-application earlier in the corner, sometimes while brake pressure is still present. The overlap between throttle and brake is intentional — a cornering tool, not a mistake.

# Plot 6 — Trail Braking Gate
Driver 3 shows 64% of the lap with both channels above 5% — 45.4 seconds across 33 zones. Still the brake position sensor residual. The genuine trail braking events are the sustained zones above a second, concentrated at the primary braking markers. The rest is sensor noise.
Driver 4 shows 5.1 seconds across 37 zones. Thirty-seven zones for 5.1 seconds gives an average zone length of 0.14 seconds. That's not trail braking — that's the brake sensor residual flickering the gate open briefly whenever the throttle rises above 5%. The real events are the longer zones at the primary corners.
Driver 5 shows 3.5 seconds across 16 zones — the cleanest reading of the three. Some of those 16 are still artefacts, but the genuine zones are identifiable — sustained signals at the primary hairpin entries where the pressure data confirms simultaneous hydraulic pressure and partial throttle.
The most useful thing this plot reveals isn't the numbers — it's the position of Driver 5's genuine trail braking zones on the distance axis. They occur later in the braking event than the other two drivers. Deeper into the corner. That's not just trail braking — that's trail braking in the part of the corner where it actually shifts load to the front axle and allows a tighter line.

# Plot 7 — Throttle Derivative
Driver 5's derivative panel has a distinctive shape — long flat near-zero regions with sharp spikes at specific points. The flat regions are the continuous partial throttle zones where the signal is changing slowly. The spikes are the precise transition moments where Driver 5 commits from corner to straight, or shuts off cleanly for braking.
Maximum opening rate of 630 %/s — highest of the three. Maximum closing rate of -1,060 %/s — also highest. Mean opening rate of 47 %/s — lowest. High peaks, low mean. Smooth everywhere, sharp at the moments that matter. That combination is the whole technique in three numbers.
Driver 3's panel is busier. More frequent moderate spikes spread across the lap rather than concentrated at transition points. Maximum opening rate of only 394 %/s — the lowest of the three. When Driver 3 commits to the throttle, they do so less decisively than either other driver. The mean of 71 %/s is the highest, but only because there's more frequent smaller movement rather than fewer larger committed inputs. More activity, less precision.
Driver 4 sits between the two. The derivative panel shows a cleaner binary structure than Driver 3 — defined open and close events that align clearly with corner exit points on the circuit. The inputs are circuit-referenced, not reactive. That's a more disciplined technique than Driver 3, but without the subtlety of Driver 5's continuous modulation.

# Plot 8 — Steering Derivative
Driver 3's panel shows maximum right rate of 631 deg/s and maximum left of -1,200 deg/s. The left rate is 90% higher than the right. Driver 3 is nearly twice as aggressive turning into left-hand corners as right-hand corners. On a circuit where the major braking zones feed left-hand hairpins, those left-hand entries are significantly more violent — higher risk of transient oversteer — while the right-hand entries are comparatively smooth. That asymmetry is a weakness that the circuit layout directly punishes.
Driver 4 shows 592 deg/s right, -562 deg/s left. Near-perfect symmetry. Driver 4 applies the same aggression to both directions, which means no directional weakness and no directional strength either. Consistent and balanced — but balanced at an aggression level that doesn't produce the fastest corner speeds.
Driver 5 shows 649 deg/s right, -532 deg/s left. Mild asymmetry, but in the opposite direction to Driver 3 — slightly more aggressive turning right. Right-hand turn-ins on this circuit enter the left-hand hairpins that lead to the main straight. The most performance-critical corners on the lap. Driver 5 is applying the highest steering rates at the corners where the most time is available. That's not an asymmetric habit — it's deliberate prioritisation.
Mean absolute steering rates — Driver 4 at 57 deg/s, Driver 3 at 54 deg/s, Driver 5 at 52 deg/s. Driver 5 uses the least average steering input while carrying the highest corner speeds. Less correction needed because the line was planned correctly, not adjusted reactively.

# Plot 9 — GG Diagram Overlay
Three overlapping clouds, three dashed envelope circles. Driver 3's cloud extends furthest at 5.314g — reflecting either genuine higher physical loading or a different sensor calibration. Driver 4 and Driver 5 sit at 2.562g and 2.809g respectively. The accelerometer scaling concern means Driver 3's absolute values can't be directly compared to the other two, but the Driver 4 to Driver 5 comparison is valid — same equipment, same potential scaling issue.
Driver 5's envelope of 2.809g is larger than Driver 4's 2.562g. Mean combined G of 1.206g versus Driver 4's 1.124g. Driver 5 loads the tyres harder on average across the lap. Faster and harder on the tyres simultaneously — which is what you'd expect from a technique built around maintaining continuous load rather than cycling between loaded and unloaded states.
The quadrant that tells the clearest story is acceleration-plus-right-turn. Driver 5 spends 21.6 seconds there — more than any other driver. That quadrant is corner exit from left-hand hairpins onto the straights. More time there means more time on power while still turning. That's the partial throttle technique showing up as physical proof in the GG diagram — not just a number on a throttle channel, but actual tyre loading data confirming the car is being driven in combined grip for longer than any other driver.
Driver 3's heaviest loading falls in the braking-plus-left quadrant — hard into left corners, aggressive turn-in, then working to recover speed through the apex. Committing to the braking event rather than the corner speed. The steering asymmetry from Plot 8 and the braking G distribution here tell exactly the same story from two different sensors.

## Conclusion — Who is Fastest and Why
Driver 5. Best lap 1:08.5 — 1.3 seconds clear of Driver 4, 2.7 seconds clear of Driver 3.
The reason isn't one channel or one corner. It's a technique philosophy that compounds across every corner of every lap.
Driver 5 treats each corner as one continuous event — throttle, brake, and steering all active simultaneously throughout, managing the tyre as a shared resource between lateral and longitudinal forces rather than switching between them sequentially. The 2.1 seconds of full throttle isn't a weakness. It's the consequence of spending the rest of the lap managing combined grip so effectively that a full-throttle straight barely exists — the car is already at speed before it gets there. The 61 km/h minimum corner speed is where that philosophy becomes a number on a screen.
Driver 4 is genuinely quick — 1.3 seconds back — and the technique is disciplined within its own logic. Symmetric steering, clean threshold braking, the highest full-throttle commitment. The problem is minimum corner speed at 54 km/h — the lowest of the three. On a circuit where over half the lap is spent cornering, sacrificing minimum speed to gain braking commitment is a trade that doesn't pay off at this level. Each corner exit starts from a lower base and the short straights aren't long enough to fully recover what was lost.
Driver 3 generates competitive straight-line pace but the data across every channel tells the same story — the inputs are working hard without producing the lap time that should follow. Highest wheelspin duration, most asymmetric steering, hottest engine temperatures despite the slowest lap times. Heat without performance is the clearest data signature of a technique that's generating friction rather than speed. The engine is paying the cost. The stopwatch isn't reflecting it.
This qualifying session was decided in the slow corners. Driver 5 flows through them. Driver 4 stops and restarts at them. Driver 3 fights them.











## RACE
# Plot 1 — Race Lap Time Progression
Driver 5 is simply the fastest driver in this race. Best lap of 70.7 seconds against Driver 4's 71.9 and Driver 3's 74.0. That 3.3 second gap to Driver 3 might not feel massive when you say it out loud, but run it over 20 laps and Driver 5 is more than a minute up the road. At that point the race is over — you're not coming back from a minute.
What I found more interesting than the raw times is the trend across the race. All three drivers got quicker as the laps went on, which is what you'd expect — tyres come in, the driver gets braver, the fuel burns off and the car lightens up. Driver 4 improved the most, dropping 6.2 seconds between their opening five laps and their closing five. Driver 3 found nearly 5 seconds, Driver 5 just under 5 as well. But here's the thing — Driver 5 was already ahead when the improvement started. They weren't chasing anyone down. They just got faster from a position that was already comfortable.
Driver 3's lap time range of 23.4 seconds from slowest to fastest is what stands out to me. That's a big window. Something was going wrong — whether that's incidents, traffic, or just inconsistent technique under pressure, I can't tell yet from this plot alone. Driver 5's range of 11.6 seconds is roughly half that. That gap in consistency is arguably more telling than the outright pace difference.

# Plot 5 — Throttle and Brake Overlay
Driver 3 is the first thing you notice. In qualifying they were flat on the throttle for 27 seconds — 38% of the lap. In the race that's down to 6 seconds. The sustained full-throttle bursts on every straight are basically gone.
That doesn't happen by choice. When a driver stops committing to the throttle that dramatically, the rear of the car has stopped giving them what they need. The trust is gone. So instead of pinning it, they're feeding the power in carefully, feeling for grip that may or may not be there. It's the data signature of a driver managing a problem they can't fully solve.
Driver 4 barely changes — 28.8 seconds full throttle in qualifying, 24.7 in the race. Four seconds less, which is about right for fuel load and tyre wear. The same binary open-close character is still there, just with the edges slightly softened for a heavier car.

The 0.5 seconds just confirms the continuous partial-throttle technique is being managed even more conservatively over a race distance. Preserving the tyres rather than extracting everything from them on one lap.
The average speed numbers say the rest. Driver 3 drops 5 km/h from qualifying to race. Driver 4 drops 4. Driver 5 drops 4 too but from a higher base — and holds the tightest drop proportionally. The technique that manages load continuously survives race conditions better than the technique that commits fully and then lifts.
On the brakes — Driver 3's on-brake time reads 98% of the lap, which is still the sensor residual. Driver 4's jumps to 60.7 seconds from qualifying's 26, a real increase driven by traffic and defensive driving. Driver 5 holds at 26 seconds. Almost identical to qualifying. Most consistent braking profile in the field, both sessions.

# Plot 6 — Trail Braking Gate
Driver 4 is where the biggest story is here. In qualifying — 5.1 seconds of trail braking, mostly sensor noise. In the race — 34.1 seconds across 69 zones. That's 47% of the lap. You can't explain that away with sensor artefacts.
What's happening is Driver 4 is using trail braking as a correction tool rather than a planned technique. The car is heavier on fuel, the tyres are degrading, corners are being approached with less precision under traffic — and when a car starts feeling unstable at corner entry, the instinctive response is to hold partial brake while bleeding in some throttle to redistribute weight and settle the rear. Driver 4 is doing that correction more and more frequently across the race. Sixty-nine zones at half a second each tells you it's reactive. Nobody plans 69 trail braking zones in one lap.
Driver 3 barely changes — 45 seconds qualifying, 46 seconds race. Sensor residual still doing most of the talking. The zones are fewer but longer, which is consistent with a driver entering corners slower and leaning on the braking load to rotate the car for the apex, because the corner speed isn't there to do it naturally.
Driver 5 actually reduces trail braking in the race — 3.5 seconds in qualifying down to 2.5. On degrading tyres, overlapping throttle and brake is a riskier move than on fresh rubber. Driver 5 recognises that and pulls back. Still using it at the specific corners where it matters. Just not as freely.

# Plot 7 — Throttle Derivative
Driver 3's maximum opening rate jumps from 394 %/s in qualifying to 733 %/s in the race. Biggest change of the three by a long way. But the mean rate barely moves. High peak, similar mean — which tells you those aggressive inputs aren't spread across the lap, they're concentrated at specific moments. Almost certainly overtaking attempts, or corners where Driver 3 goes for it, finds wheelspin, and has to immediately back off. The spike and the correction. You can see the story in the shape of the trace without even looking at the numbers.
Driver 4 does the opposite — maximum opening rate actually drops from qualifying to race. Less aggressive at the snap, slightly more frequent overall. That's the sensible adaptation. Reduce the inputs that cause wheelspin on degrading tyres, increase the frequency of moderate inputs to keep the car moving through the lap. It's a reasonable adjustment.
Driver 5's peak opening rate drops from 630 to 513 %/s. Deliberate. But the mean — 48 %/s in the race against 47 %/s in qualifying — is essentially identical. That's the number that matters. The peak is softer because the tyres need it to be. The average behaviour hasn't changed at all. That's what technique consistency actually looks like across sessions.
One thing visible across all three panels — the largest throttle spikes occur at the same positions on track for every driver. The hairpin exits. Everyone puts the power down in the same places. The difference is what happens after the spike. Driver 3 shows oscillation — the throttle spike, then corrections as wheelspin hits. Driver 4 shows clean single peaks. Driver 5's spikes are sharp and then immediately flat. Applied it, it stuck, moved on.

# Plot 8 — Steering Derivative
Driver 3's steering data between qualifying and race doesn't just change — it flips completely. In qualifying the left-hand rate dominated at -1,200 deg/s, right side at 631. In the race, right dominates at 1,310 deg/s, left drops to -643. The most aggressive side has swapped entirely.
That's not a setup adjustment or a strategic choice. That's a car that changed underneath the driver mid-race and a driver who ran out of answers for it. The most likely cause is tyre degradation on the left-rear — the tyre that takes the hardest load through this circuit's right-hand braking and left-hand cornering sequences. As the left-rear goes away, the car stops rotating cleanly on left-hand entries. Driver 3's response is to reduce aggression on the left side to stop making it worse, and overcompensate on the right side trying to find rotation elsewhere. It works partially. It doesn't fix the lap time.
Driver 4's left-hand steering rate increases 73% from qualifying to race. Right goes up too but less dramatically. The car is heavier on fuel and turns in slower, so Driver 4 commits more aggressively on the left entries to get the same rotation. More active steering across the lap generally — compensating for a car that's harder to rotate than it was in qualifying.
Driver 5 goes more aggressive in both directions in the race — right side up from 649 to 872, left side from -532 to -760. Both directions, more committed. That's the opposite of a driver struggling. That's a driver who's found the race balance and is using more decisive turn-in to hold qualifying corner speeds against the additional fuel load. Left-hand input time increases to 34 seconds in the race. Those are the hairpins that lead to the main straight. Driver 5 is spending more time and more commitment exactly where the lap time is.

## Conclusion Race — Who is Fastest and Why
The Result
Driver 5 won this race and it wasn't particularly close by the end.
Best lap of 70.7 seconds, lap spread of just 11.6 seconds across the whole race, lowest wheelspin, highest average speed, tightest consistency. Pick any metric that actually matters and Driver 5 is either first or so close to first it makes no difference. This wasn't a lucky result — it was inevitable from about lap three onwards.
Driver 4 is second at 71.9 seconds best lap. Quick enough to be in the conversation on a single lap, but the race distance exposed something. The consistency just wasn't there — a standard deviation of 2.55 seconds per lap means some laps were good and some weren't, and in a race that costs you positions and puts you in traffic at the wrong moments.
Driver 3 is third at 74.0 seconds, 3.3 seconds a lap off Driver 5. The drop from qualifying to race was the biggest of anyone here. Whatever was working in qualifying, stopped working in the race, and the data tells that story pretty clearly.

# Why Driver 5 Won
There isn't one reason. It builds across everything.
Start with Plot 1. Driver 5 improved by nearly 5 seconds from their opening laps to their closing laps — almost the same improvement as Driver 3. The difference is where they started from. Driver 5 was already 4 seconds faster at the beginning of the race and improved from there. The heatmap just shows a row getting quietly greener lap after lap. No disasters, no recovery laps, no moments where something went wrong. Just relentless forward progress from a position of control.
Plot 2 shows something that took me a second look to fully appreciate. Driver 5's average speed is the highest in the race, but their minimum corner speed sits between Driver 4 and Driver 3 — not the fastest at that specific measurement. So where does the average speed come from? It comes from not losing time anywhere. There's no section of this circuit where Driver 5 is clearly off the pace. The gap isn't built in one place — it accumulates from dozens of tiny margins that never swing the other way.
Plot 3 makes it visual. The delta between Driver 5 and Driver 3 just grows from the start line and never comes back. No oscillation, no section where Driver 3 pulls any of it back. Driver 5 versus Driver 4 is more interesting — Driver 4 actually has a genuine advantage at the main braking zone, arriving faster and hitting the brakes harder. But that advantage disappears within about 300 metres of track as the corner exit speed takes over. The better braker doesn't win the lap. The better corner exit does.
Then Plot 5, which is the one that always gets a reaction. Driver 5 uses half a second of full throttle in the race. Half a second. In a 70 second lap. Driver 4 uses 34% of the lap at full throttle. Driver 3 uses 9%. The fastest driver by a clear margin uses almost no flat throttle, and that's not a mistake in the data.
What's happening is that Driver 5 is already on partial throttle throughout the corner — blending the power in while the car is still turning, managing the balance between cornering force and acceleration simultaneously. The car exits each corner at a speed that means the straight barely needs to exist. There's no moment of "right, straight now, full power" because the speed was never fully lost in the first place. The throttle is never pinned because it was never fully lifted.
Plot 6 shows how the race distance changes things for each driver. Driver 4's trail braking goes from 5 seconds in qualifying to 34 seconds in the race. That's not a technique choice — that's constant corner-by-corner correction. The car under race load isn't behaving the way Driver 4 needs it to, so every entry becomes a small crisis that has to be managed on the fly. Driver 5's trail braking actually goes down in the race compared to qualifying. Less correction needed, not more. Because the technique doesn't create problems that need correcting.
Plot 7 is where you see what pressure does to each driver. Driver 3's peak throttle opening rate nearly doubles from qualifying to race. At first that sounds like more commitment, but combine it with 18 seconds of wheelspin and the picture becomes clear — those aggressive throttle inputs aren't planned, they're reactions. Something has already gone wrong and the driver is responding to it. Driver 5's average throttle opening rate barely changes between sessions. 47 in qualifying, 48 in the race. Essentially the same. The technique doesn't shift under pressure because it doesn't need to.
Plot 8 has the steering story and it's a telling one. Driver 5 gets more aggressive in both directions from qualifying to race — not because something's wrong, but because the car has more fuel load and needs more committed inputs to achieve the same rotation. That's proactive adaptation — understanding what the car needs and giving it that. Driver 3's steering asymmetry reverses completely between sessions. In qualifying the left side dominated. In the race the right side dominates. That's not a plan. That's a driver whose car changed underneath them during the race and who never found a clean answer to it.
Plot 9 closes it out. Driver 5 spends the most time in the quadrant where the car is on power while still turning — the combined load phase that is the whole point of their technique. Driver 4 spends the most time in the braking-while-cornering quadrant. Both are real, both are legitimate. But on a circuit where over half the lap is spent in slow corners and the straights are barely long enough to breathe, corner speed beats braking commitment. The data just confirms what the lap times already showed.

# The One Real Reason
Driver 5 carries speed through corners. Driver 4 and Driver 3 shed speed at corners and spend the straights recovering it. On a circuit this short with straights this brief, there simply isn't enough room to recover what the corners take away.
